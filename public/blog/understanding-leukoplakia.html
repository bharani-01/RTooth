Understanding Leukoplakia & Erythroplakia: Early Warning Signs

By Dr. Sarah Jenkins, DDS
June 15, 2026
5 min read

Early identification of pre-cancerous changes in the oral cavity is one of the most effective ways to prevent advanced oral cancers. Among the most common oral lesions encountered by dental practitioners are Leukoplakia and Erythroplakia. These lesions are classified as potentially malignant oral disorders (PMODs), meaning they have an increased risk of progressing to squamous cell carcinoma.

What is Leukoplakia?

Oral leukoplakia is defined by the World Health Organization as a white patch or plaque that cannot be characterized clinically or pathologically as any other disease. It is predominantly caused by chronic mucosal irritation, most frequently linked to tobacco use (both smoked and smokeless) and heavy alcohol consumption.

Leukoplakia presents in two primary clinical forms:

  • Homogeneous Leukoplakia: A uniformly thin, flat white patch with smooth, wrinkled, or finely cracked surfaces. These lesions carry a lower risk of malignancy (typically 1-5%).
  • Non-homogeneous Leukoplakia: A white and red lesion (sometimes called erythroleukoplakia) with an irregular, nodular, or verrucous (wart-like) surface. The risk of malignancy is significantly higher in these lesions, often exceeding 15-20%.
"Any white patch in the oral cavity that persists for more than 14 days without an obvious local cause (such as a sharp tooth cusp) must be biopsied."

What is Erythroplakia?

Erythroplakia is a bright red, velvety patch in the oral cavity that cannot be clinically or pathologically diagnosed as any other inflammatory or infectious condition. Unlike leukoplakia, erythroplakia is much less common but carries an exceptionally high malignancy risk. Research shows that up to 90% of erythroplakias represent severe epithelial dysplasia, carcinoma in situ, or early invasive squamous cell carcinoma at the time of initial biopsy.

Diagnostic Protocols & Staging

When a patient presents with a suspicious lesion, oncologists and dental specialists follow a structured pathway:

  1. Clinical Assessment: Identify lesion site, borders, texturing, and size. High-risk anatomical areas include the lateral borders of the tongue, the floor of the mouth, and the soft palate.
  2. Eliminate Local Irritants: Smooth any sharp teeth or restore ill-fitting dentures. If the lesion is reactive, it should shrink or disappear within 10-14 days.
  3. Tissue Biopsy: If the lesion persists, a punch or incisional biopsy is performed. Histopathology is the gold standard to evaluate the degree of epithelial dysplasia (Mild, Moderate, Severe, or Carcinoma in situ).
